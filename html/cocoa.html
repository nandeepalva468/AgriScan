Cocoa Diseases & Symptoms

Symptoms

Early signs include translucent spots on the surface of cocoa pods that develop into small, dark, hard patches. These lesions rapidly expand and within two weeks, the pod may turn entirely black and necrotic. White to yellow downy growth appears on these blackened areas. Internally, the tissue dries out, leading to mummified pods with no viable seeds.

Cause

The disease is caused by oomycetes such as Phytophthora megakarya and Phytophthora capsici. These pathogens thrive in moist, warm climates and spread quickly through rain splash and infected tools.

Description

Cocoa pod rot is one of the most severe diseases affecting cocoa plantations. P. megakarya is more aggressive and prevalent in West and Central Africa, while P. capsici is more common in Central and South America. If not controlled, this disease can cause up to 100% loss in infected pods, making it a major concern for cocoa growers.

Management

Effective control includes spraying copper-based fungicides (e.g., Bordeaux mixture) and systemic fungicides regularly, especially before and during rainy seasons. Maintain good air circulation by proper spacing and pruning. Remove and destroy infected and mummified pods promptly. Use disease-resistant cocoa varieties where available.
